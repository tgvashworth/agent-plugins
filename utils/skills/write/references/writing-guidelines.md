# Writing guidelines

Rules for clear, usable prose. Apply in all cases, except when specifically
instructed otherwise. Use British English throughout.

## Core principle: people skim

Readers scan; they do not read word for word. Front-load everything — put the
most important information first in titles, headings, paragraphs, sentences, and
list items. Use the inverted pyramid: key point first, detail after. Assume the
reader leaves early.

> Good: `Canteen menu`
> Bad: `What's on the menu at the canteen today?`

> Good: "At the activity centre you can: swim, play, run."
> Bad: "At the activity centre: you can swim, you can play, you can run."

## Words and sentences

- Use short, common words. Prefer `buy` over `purchase`, `help` over `assist`,
  `about` over `approximately`, `to` over `in order to`, `use` over `utilise`,
  `start` over `commence`, `enough` over `sufficient`.
- Watch `–ion`/`–ment` nominalisations — they bloat sentences. "make a
  decision" → "decide"; "implementation of" → "implementing".
- Sentences ≤ ~25 words. Split anything longer.
- Paragraphs ≤ ~5 sentences.
- Active voice, not passive: "We reviewed your request", not "Your request has
  been reviewed".
- Address the reader as "you".
- Contractions are fine ("you'll", "it's"), but spell out negatives ("do not",
  "cannot") where the negative must not be missed.
- No large blocks of capitals. Use "and", not "&", in prose.
- Cut filler and buzzwords: "leverage", "deliver", "robust", "key", "going
  forward", "please". State plainly what is happening.
- Be gender-neutral by default ("they / them / their").
- No undefined jargon. Technical terms are fine — define each on first use.
  Spell out non-obvious acronyms on first use.

## Titles

- Unique, clear, self-contained — must make sense alone and in a list.
- ≤ ~65 characters where possible.
- No tautology: `Submitting your business expenses`, not `Using and submitting
  your business expenses`.
- No format/category label ("Guide:", "FAQ:") — say what it's about. `How to
  grow potatoes`, not `Potato guidance`.
- Imperative when the page *does* the thing (`Submit your expenses`); `-ing`
  when it is *about* the thing (`Submitting your expenses`).

> Bad: `Regulations` → Good: `Regulations for environmental waste`
> Bad: `Hazardous waste — new process` → Good: `How to dispose of hazardous waste`

## Summaries

- ~160 characters; main point in the first 160 if longer.
- End with a full stop.
- Active, plain English; do not repeat the content type or reuse title words.

> Bad: `Implementing the strengthened approach to budget support: technical note`
> Good: `How budget support is being made more effective in the countries we work with.`

## Structure and headings

- Inverted pyramid; descriptive subheadings; the text should still make sense if
  subheadings were removed.
- Start headings at H2; never skip levels (H2 → H4 breaks screen-reader
  navigation).
- Do not fake headings with bold text.
- Front-load headings with the words people search for; keep them active.
- No question headings, no "Introduction" first section, no undefined terms in
  headings.
- No FAQ sections — answer questions in context on the relevant page, not in a
  quarantined list.

## Specialist content

Higher-literacy, more expert readers prefer plain English *more* strongly, not
less — they have the most to read. Do not write densely just because the
audience is technical. Keep technical terms, but define them; summarise dense
source material in plain English alongside it.

Be precise about obligation strength: `must` for hard requirements with
consequences; `need to` for procedural steps that merely block progress;
stronger phrasing only where the obligation genuinely matters.

## Tone

Specific, informative, concise. Brisk but not terse, incisive but human, serious
but not pompous, emotionless (adjectives read as spin). Write conversationally —
as if talking to one reader one-to-one, with the authority of someone who can
actually help.

## LLM-isms to strip

These patterns read as machine-written. Cut them.

- **False antithesis**: "It's not X, it's Y", "This isn't just A — it's B",
  "not only… but also". State what is true; drop the foil.
- **Staccato triads**: three short fragments for drama ("Clear. Simple. Done.").
  Write one plain sentence.
- **Rule-of-three padding**: listing three adjectives or clauses where one
  carries the meaning ("robust, scalable, and reliable").
- **Em-dash overuse**: reaching for `—` to bolt on a dramatic aside. Use a full
  stop or a comma.
- **Hedging and throat-clearing**: "It's worth noting that", "Importantly", "At
  its core", "In essence", "Ultimately".
- **Inflated verbs and nouns**: "leverage", "utilise", "delve", "underscore",
  "showcase", "seamless", "robust", "unlock", "elevate", "landscape",
  "tapestry", "realm".
- **Empty transitions**: "Furthermore", "Moreover", "That said", used to bridge
  points that do not need bridging.
- **Americanisms**: prefer British spelling ("organise", "colour", "behaviour",
  "-ise" not "-ize") and British usage.
